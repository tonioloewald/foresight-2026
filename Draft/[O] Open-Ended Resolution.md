# Open-Ended Resolution

*One-pager. The One Rule resolves a task in isolation. But tasks rarely stand alone — one builds on another, or fights against it. This page is how results **chain**: how to **build on** a prior result, and how to **oppose** one. It's foreshadowed everywhere (parries, persuasion, hacking, a forged document used later) and it's all the same two moves.*

## Opposing — both roll, the QRs fight

When two characters contest directly — a parry against an attack, stealth against a searcher, a tug-of-war, a chess match, a lawyer building a case against one tearing it down — **both roll their own task and compare Quality Ratings. The better QR prevails.**

- A **large** QR gap is a decisive win; a **small** gap is a narrow one.
- **Equal QRs cancel** — and that's a feature, not a fudge. This is the blow that *just barely* gets blocked, the tail that's almost spotted, the point neither player can convert. Nothing decisive happens, the tension holds, and **you keep going.** A string of ties is a long, taut struggle.
- If you genuinely must break a tie (someone has to act *now*), fall back on the **ability scores** — the more skilled contestant edges it.

> *Older editions had you use your opponent's QR as your ease factor. Rolling and comparing QRs is simpler, more symmetric, and produces those satisfying near-stalemates the old way couldn't.*

## How much got through — partial blocking

Sometimes you don't want win/lose but *how much* of an effort survived a partial defense (an attack partly parried, a case partly undermined). For an *effective* defense the rule is simple arithmetic: a defense **equal to or better than** the attack negates it; otherwise the attack's QR drops **(5 − the defending QR)** notches down the 1-2-3-4 scale. The failure end isn't arithmetic — it follows the general principle that **QR7 is plain failure, QR10 actively backfires**: a QR7 defense is *no defense* (the attack lands at its full rolled QR), and a QR10 defense **makes the attack worse** by one notch (the defender fumbled into it). The result is the attack's **effective** QR — what got through.

| Attack ↓ \ Defense → | QR1 | QR2 | QR3 | QR4 | QR7 (fail) | QR10 (botch) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **QR1** | blocked | QR4 | QR3 | QR2 | QR1 | QR1 |
| **QR2** | blocked | blocked | QR4 | QR3 | QR2 | QR1 |
| **QR3** | blocked | blocked | blocked | QR4 | QR3 | QR2 |
| **QR4** | blocked | blocked | blocked | blocked | QR4 | QR3 |

*(The better the defense, the more of the attack it eats; an equal defense blocks it cleanly — the just-barely parry. A botched defense leaves you worse off than no defense at all.)*

## Building — a modifier, not a new base

When one task's product feeds a later task — a tuned engine you then race, a forged passport you then present, intel you then act on — carry the first result forward as an **ease-factor modifier** on the follow-up:

> **Build: +(5 − QR).** QR1 → +4, QR2 → +3, QR3 → +2, QR4 → +1. Opposing a standing prior result instead? Take the same value as a **penalty**.

Crucially this is a **modifier**, not a replacement base ease factor: the follow-up still uses *its own* ability and BEF, nudged up or down by what came before. Even a marginal success (QR4) leaves the next person a little something to stand on, and a brilliant one (QR1) hands them a real head start.

## Examples

- **Sneaking vs. noticing** — your Stealth against their Perception (or Search, if they're actively looking). Compare QRs; a tie means they sense *something* but can't place it.
- **Concealing vs. finding** — hiding an object (Stealth/relevant skill) against a searcher; a tie is "they walked right past it."
- **Attack vs. parry** — the defender's parry blocks by the table above; an equal QR is the blow turned aside at the last instant.
- **Hacking vs. security** — the defender *built* the system earlier, so the intruder works against a standing result: apply −(5 − the setup's QR) to the hack. (A botched install, conversely, helps the attacker.)

**Tennis (and why this rule is general).** Each point is a contest of Tennis skill, compared by QR. The server gets **+1** (the serve advantage). Either player may **commit extra energy** for a better ease factor — going for the line — at the price of an Endurance check and mounting **exhaustion** over a long match. A **tied QR is deuce**: nobody converts, replay the point, tension climbs. It maps perfectly — and the very same shape runs a football match, a dogfight, or two generals maneuvering armies. You never needed a tennis rule; you needed the One Rule and a QR comparison.

*Designer's Note: two moves — compare QRs to oppose, shift an EF to build — cover tennis, trials, hacking duels, assembly lines, and seductions alike. And note why the QR scale runs 1-2-3-4-7-10: it makes (5 − QR) come out right, so building on a botch is hard and building on brilliance is easy, with no extra table to consult.*
