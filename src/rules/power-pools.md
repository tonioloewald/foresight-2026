# Power Pools
<!--{ "parent": "conflict", "order": 6 }-->

*One-pager. Magic, divine intervention, and mystic disciplines all draw on a **power pool** — but a pool isn't a counter you spend down, it's an **exhaustion track** exactly like fatigue. You don't tally points; you roll to **gather** power, and a track of exhaustion grows when you reach too hard. See Wounds & Recovery for the parent mechanism; this page is the magical sibling.*

## Gathering power

Every power source has a **gather skill** — *Mana* for magic, *Piety* for divine power, *Spirit* for mystic disciplines (see those chapters). Using a power means first **gathering** for it: a gather roll whose ease factor reflects how much you're asking for (a candle-flame is trivial; a firestorm is brutal). What you do with the power once gathered — the *weaving* — is a separate matter, governed by the relevant chapter.

The gather roll reads off its QR like any task:

- **Solid success** — the power comes, free and clear.
- **Marginal success / failure** — the power still comes, but you **gain a level of power-pool exhaustion** (you scraped the bottom of the barrel).
- **Disastrous failure (QR10)** — the power **fizzles** *and* you take a level of exhaustion. You reached hard and came up empty.

The GM may also rule that especially demanding effects cost a level of exhaustion **automatically**, no roll — some things simply can't be done without burning yourself.

## Pushing it

A caster may **volunteer** to take one level of power-pool exhaustion for a one-off **+4** modifier to a gather (or to the effect). This is reaching past your limit on purpose — reliable, but it stacks up fast and the bill comes due.

## Power-pool exhaustion

Each source has **its own** exhaustion track (your Mana can be wrung dry while your Spirit is fresh). A track penalizes use of **its own** source at full value and everything else at half, and tracks **stack** with fatigue and each other (see Wounds & Recovery for the full rule and worked example). So gather rolls get harder the more spent you are — the death-spiral that stops a mage from trivially chaining world-shaking spells — and a drained wizard is also a worse swordsman and a duller conversationalist.

Recovery works just like fatigue (Wounds & Recovery): you clear **one level at a time, per track**, and deeper levels need more rest.

| Clearing a level at depth… | …takes |
|---|---|
| 1 | A 15-minute breather (meditation, prayer) |
| 2 | A long break |
| 3 | A good night's sleep |
| 4 | Unconsciousness / near-coma |
| 5+ | Special intervention (a holy site, a ley line, a master's aid) |

When several tracks are spent at once, a given rest shifts one level on one track — sort the rest out with more rest. (Some sources recover by other means than rest — **Piety**, for instance, is rebuilt through acts of devotion and sacrifice rather than sleep; see Religion.) As with fatigue, runaway power-pool exhaustion (four or more) risks converting into real **wounds**: magic burns the body that channels it.

*Designer's Note: this keeps the two-step heart of the magic system — gather, then weave — intact and balanced, while throwing out the bookkeeping. The gather roll is the same roll that meters exhaustion, so there's no separate pool to track; and "spend a level for +4" gives desperate casters a real, costly lever instead of a hard wall.*
