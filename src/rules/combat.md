# Combat
<!--{ "parent": "conflict", "order": 1 }-->

*One-pager. The whole of combat is the One Rule plus two questions: **when do you act?** (Initiative) and **how much can you do at once?** (Simultaneous Action). Everything below is those two ideas applied.*

Combat runs in **rounds** — brief bursts of violence separated by waiting for someone to make a move. There is no action-point budget, and no list of action types to memorise.

**A round is as long as the contest's tempo demands.** Direct personal combat is roughly 3–5 seconds a round, with random-length lulls between bursts (ducked behind cover, catching your breath). Slower contests run longer rounds — see Pursuit. Don't track the clock; the GM sets the tempo to fit the scene.

The round is the **quantum**: Initiative cuts time into rounds and decides who moves within one, and Simultaneous Action prices whatever you try to cram into one. The two ideas are the same idea seen from opposite ends, which is why the pair covers the whole of combat between them.

## Initiative — when do you act?

### Awareness: from narrative to "shit going down"

The hard, real moment is the transition out of story-telling into a fight. Every character starts that moment somewhere on a spectrum:

> **Surprised** (woken by the attack) → **Startled** → **Alert** → **Ready** → **Lying in wait** (the ambusher)

Awareness sets your **Initiative**. Coarse, obvious effects modify it: a visored helmet or a blind corner is a disadvantage; a sniper studying the scene in perfect safety is at a large advantage. Once the fight is live, awareness tends to level out to simple questions — chiefly, *can you see what's happening?*

### The Round

1. **Roll Initiative** (awareness-driven). Order everyone best to worst; ties broken by score.
2. **Declare, in order, act or wait.** Acting characters resolve their action when declared. Waiting characters hold for a trigger.
3. **Waiting persists.** Anyone still waiting from last round stays waiting until their declaration comes up again. *The totally unaware get no declaration on the first round at all — which is why ambushes are murder.*

### Reaction & Pre-emption

When a waiting character reacts to something (usually someone else acting), they may act **immediately after** it — or try to **pre-empt** and go **just before**.

> **Pre-emption = a Coordination roll, modified by your Initiative QR**, ties resolved by Initiative Score. Reflexes do the work; awareness only biases the odds — no good having lightning reflexes if you're confused. Succeed and you go just before; fail and you go just after. It's still a roll, so even a superbly coordinated, well-placed shooter keeps real uncertainty.

A pre-emption can itself be pre-empted (the counter-sniper), but you'll rarely go deeper than that.

> **You never know for certain you'll act first.** You can be pretty sure — but not certain. This is the whole game. The fast gunslinger *probably* draws first, the sniper *probably* fires before the hostage-taker reacts; neither is guaranteed. It's exactly why a police sniper in a standoff is loath to just shoot the other sniper. Remove that uncertainty and every gunfight is just "highest number wins" — which is why these scenes don't work in other games and do work here.

*E.g. an archer waits, trigger: "the mage does something." The mage starts a spell. The archer pre-empts (QR of Initiative); on success the arrow lands before the spell goes off — but a confident archer can still be a hair too slow.*

## Simultaneous Action — how much do you get done?

A turn's natural shape is **advance–attack–retreat**, or **pop out–fire–pull back**. How well any of it goes depends on one thing: how much of your attention it got.

> **Every task takes −1 for each *other* task you're attempting, and −1 more for each task you put *ahead* of it.**

Declare your tasks in priority order and read off the ladder:

| You are attempting | 1st | 2nd | 3rd | 4th |
|---|:--:|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| **one thing**, with total focus | 0 | | | |
| **two things** | −1 | −2 | | |
| **three things** | −2 | −3 | −4 | |
| **four things** | −3 | −4 | −5 | −6 |

Take the modifier as an **ease factor** hit (you do it *worse*) or as a hit to **yield** (you do *less* of it) — whichever the task makes sense of. Read one column, as always: fast *or* good *or* much.

### What counts as a task

**A task is a thing you want rolled.** That is the whole test, and it polices itself. A step, a shouted word, drawing a weapon, aiming — these aren't tasks; they happen, with their honest consequences (pop up to fire and you're still half-exposed when the round ends). There are still no "free actions": a thing is either of no consequence, or it isn't free. But it only costs you *attention* if you wanted it to go well enough to roll for it.

So *advance–attack–retreat* is one task — the attack — and takes no penalty. Nobody has to adjudicate anything. Decline to make your movement a task and you simply move: noisily, visibly, and at the GM's discretion.

> *A sniper on a stable platform, tracking an unaware target, doing nothing else: one thing, total focus, no modifier. A lone officer advancing through a dark basement — trying to work out where the enemy is, keep her weapon on target, **and** move quietly — is attempting three things, at −2/−3/−4, on top of a situation that was already against her. She is not going to find him. That is the correct answer; she survives that scene because he cocks his revolver, not because she rolled well.*

### Help sheds tasks

A helper doesn't hand you a bonus — they **take a task off your list**, and the ladder does the rest.

*A sniper ranging his own shot and firing is attempting two things: the shot is at −1. Give him a spotter who owns the ranging, and he is down to one thing, at 0. The spotter is worth exactly +1 — and it falls out of the rule rather than being stipulated.*

Two consequences worth having. Help can never push you past your own focused best, because the ladder bottoms out at one task — so assistance cannot be stacked into absurdity. And a helper who takes nothing gives nothing. The shed task is a real task: the spotter rolls for it, and a spotter who blows the roll hands over bad numbers.

A helper who is genuinely *better* at the task than you is a separate matter — that's good information, and it earns an ordinary situational ease-factor bonus (the same family as *aimed* +1, *braced* +1). That is where a second point comes from, not from the ladder.

### Reactions: Parry, Dodge, Flinch, Duck

Reactions are tasks too — but tasks you *didn't plan*, so they're priced differently. They are **not a pool**. Take as many as you like; each carries a **cumulative −2**. Keep parrying and you simply get worse and worse at it. Skill decides how far down that ladder you stay dangerous, so the better fighter naturally gets more good reactions — no bookkeeping required.

> **The parry you saw coming is cheaper than the parry you didn't.** Tasks you *declare together* share their cost on the ladder above; a reaction you were surprised into pays the full −2 each. This is a large part of why waiting — declaring a trigger and holding for it — is worth so much.

- **Parry** (opposed): a critical parry lets you counter-attack; an overwhelming one leaves the attacker off-balance (disarm, or trade places).
- **Dodge** (opposed): **dodge and give ground** — back up a step — for a one-off advantage. This is what makes melee *move*: fighters circle, press, and yield ground organically, instead of standing toe-to-toe.
- **Flinch / duck:** if you're exposed and someone shoots, reacting back behind cover is a legitimate reaction (at the stacking penalty).

### Never pay twice

The ladder is the price of dividing your own attention. **The situation's ease factor describes the world** — range, cover, light, the target's movement — and never your own multitasking. If shooting-on-the-move already cost you −1 for being two tasks, it does *not* also take a "firing on the move" penalty. Charge it once, on the ladder, and leave it there.

## Beyond Combat

This is just **initiative and attention for any tense situation**. The quick-draw, the ambushed negotiation, the scramble to grab the artifact before the other guy — awareness, act-or-wait, pre-emption, and "how many things are you trying to do at once?" resolve all of them. Reach for this page whenever timing and nerve decide the outcome, not only when weapons come out.

Note that simultaneity is measured against the **quantum**, so the same rule scales with the tempo without a special case. Moving and watching are two tasks inside a three-second burst of gunfire; across a long pursuit turn, or an afternoon of tracking, they're two things you alternate between — one task each.

*Emergent tactics: in almost every other RPG, players concentrate fire to kill one target at a time, because a dead enemy is the only less-dangerous enemy. In ForeSight a wound is −(level) to everything and a stun takes an enemy out of the turn, so a hurt, rattled foe is dramatically less dangerous than a healthy one. After dealing with the worst threats, the smart play is to **spread** attacks across as many enemies as possible — degrade the whole group rather than execute one at a time. This falls out of the rules on its own, and it happens to mirror how real firefights are won: by suppression, not by headcount.*

*Designer's Note: the gedanken experiments that drove this whole system were always the Western shootout, the same shootout with a dastardly sniper, the Iai duel, and the hostage situation. None of them resolve correctly in any other TTRPG — the nastiest edge cases of combat, that vastly more complex systems (Aftermath, I'm looking at you) never even tried to model. They cost one page here because EF and QR do the work: every ruling is "make an Initiative roll and read its QR," or "count what you're trying to do at once." Nothing to memorize, and the probability arrows all point the right way.*
