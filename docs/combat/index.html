Combat

One-pager. The whole of combat is the One Rule plus an answer to: in a tense situation, who gets to do what, and when?

Combat runs in rounds — brief bursts of violence separated by waiting for someone to make a move. There is no action-point budget. Each round you get one primary action and one secondary action; the interesting question is when they land relative to everyone else.

A round is as long as the contest's tempo demands. Direct personal combat is roughly 3–5 seconds a round, with random-length lulls between bursts (ducked behind cover, catching your breath). Slower contests run longer rounds — see Pursuit. Don't track the clock; the GM sets the tempo to fit the scene.

Awareness: from narrative to "shit going down"

The hard, real moment is the transition out of story-telling into a fight. Every character starts that moment somewhere on a spectrum:

Surprised (woken by the attack) → Startled → Alert → Ready → Lying in wait (the ambusher)

Awareness sets your Initiative. Coarse, obvious effects modify it: a visored helmet or a blind corner is a disadvantage; a sniper studying the scene in perfect safety is at a large advantage. Once the fight is live, awareness tends to level out to simple questions — chiefly, can you see what's happening?

The Round

  1. Roll Initiative (awareness-driven). Order everyone best to worst; ties broken by score.
  2. Declare, in order, act or wait. Acting characters resolve their action when declared. Waiting characters hold for a trigger.
  3. Waiting persists. Anyone still waiting from last round stays waiting until their declaration comes up again. The totally unaware get no declaration on the first round at all — which is why ambushes are murder.

Reaction & Pre-emption

When a waiting character reacts to something (usually someone else acting), they may act immediately after it — or try to pre-empt and go just before.

Pre-emption = a Coordination roll, modified by your Initiative QR, ties resolved by Initiative Score. Reflexes do the work; awareness only biases the odds — no good having lightning reflexes if you're confused. Succeed and you go just before; fail and you go just after. It's still a roll, so even a superbly coordinated, well-placed shooter keeps real uncertainty.

A pre-emption can itself be pre-empted (the counter-sniper), but you'll rarely go deeper than that.

You never know for certain you'll act first. You can be pretty sure — but not certain. This is the whole game. The fast gunslinger probably draws first, the sniper probably fires before the hostage-taker reacts; neither is guaranteed. It's exactly why a police sniper in a standoff is loath to just shoot the other sniper. Remove that uncertainty and every gunfight is just "highest number wins" — which is why these scenes don't work in other games and do work here.

E.g. an archer waits, trigger: "the mage does something." The mage starts a spell. The archer pre-empts (QR of Initiative); on success the arrow lands before the spell goes off — but a confident archer can still be a hair too slow.

Actions

A turn's natural shape is advance–attack–retreat, or pop out–fire–pull back.

  • Primary action — the one thing you concentrate on: an attack, a spell, picking a lock under fire. You may spend it as a second secondary instead.
  • Secondary action — done without real thought: a step, aiming, a shouted word, drawing a weapon. Positional and advantage-setting — with honest tradeoffs (pop up to fire and you're still half-exposed when the round ends).

There are no "free actions." A thing is either of no consequence, or it isn't free.

Reactions: Parry, Dodge, Flinch, Duck

Reactions — parrying, dodging, flinching, ducking back to cover — are not a pool. Take as many as you like, but each carries a cumulative penalty (typically –2 each): keep parrying and you simply get worse and worse at it. Skill decides how far down that ladder you stay dangerous, so the better fighter naturally gets more good reactions — no bookkeeping required.

  • Parry (opposed): a critical parry lets you counter-attack; an overwhelming one leaves the attacker off-balance (disarm, or trade places).
  • Dodge (opposed): dodge and give ground — back up a step — for a one-off advantage. This is what makes melee move: fighters circle, press, and yield ground organically, instead of standing toe-to-toe.
  • Flinch / duck: if you're exposed and someone shoots, reacting back behind cover is a legitimate reaction (at the stacking penalty).

Beyond Combat

This is just initiative for any tense situation. The quick-draw, the ambushed negotiation, the scramble to grab the artifact before the other guy — awareness, act-or-wait, and pre-emption resolve all of them. Reach for this page whenever timing and nerve decide the outcome, not only when weapons come out.

Emergent tactics: in almost every other RPG, players concentrate fire to kill one target at a time, because a dead enemy is the only less-dangerous enemy. In ForeSight a wound is −(level) to everything and a stun takes an enemy out of the turn, so a hurt, rattled foe is dramatically less dangerous than a healthy one. After dealing with the worst threats, the smart play is to spread attacks across as many enemies as possible — degrade the whole group rather than execute one at a time. This falls out of the rules on its own, and it happens to mirror how real firefights are won: by suppression, not by headcount.

Designer's Note: the gedanken experiments that drove this whole system were always the Western shootout, the same shootout with a dastardly sniper, the Iai duel, and the hostage situation. None of them resolve correctly in any other TTRPG — the nastiest edge cases of combat, that vastly more complex systems (Aftermath, I'm looking at you) never even tried to model. They cost one page here because EF and QR do the work: every ruling is "make an Initiative roll and read its QR," or "it's a reaction, so stack the penalty." Nothing to memorize, and the probability arrows all point the right way.
